The Sun is Alive

Darwin's theory of evolution for stars

By: Justin Harvey

The laws of nature are the same everywhere. That's called universalism and it is the most important (ongoing) realization in science. This theory proposes that Darwin's laws of evolution and selection are also universal throughout the observable universe—including our sun.

The Path to Universalism

Breaking Geocentrism

First we discovered that the Earth orbits the sun, not the other way around. Our local position is not special.

Universal Physics

Later on, Newton saw that famous falling apple and asked "does the moon also fall?". Our local physics is not special.

Universal Evolution

Now I posit that Darwin's laws of evolution and selection are also universal throughout the observable universe—including celestial bodies like our sun.

Barriers to Recognition

Historical Rejection

The idea that the sun is somehow alive was specifically rejected by the church as a form of paganism.

Parochialism

The church has generally pushed parochialism (the idea that our local frame of reference is special) for thousands of years.

Vast Timescales

Cosmic timescales are unthinkably large (although it's possible that in the early universe they used to be shorter).

Observational Limitations

We can't see very clearly into the sun, and we see even less clearly into other stars.

Constellation of Supporting Ideas

The Fermi Paradox

The Fermi paradox feels intuitively like parochialism. Perhaps we're looking for the wrong type of life in the wrong places.

Stellar Lifecycle

Stars have a lifecycle including long periods of homeostasis, similar to biological organisms.

Energy Efficiency

The sun produces less heat per unit volume than the human body—a counterintuitive fact that challenges our perception of stars as simply raging furnaces.

Complex Internal Dynamics

Once a photon is emitted by the sun's core, it takes at least 10,000 years to reach the surface. This reveals complex internal dynamics inside the sun: sunspots, weather, currents, field shifts, etc. The more we study the sun, the more we realize how complex of a system it really is.

The Lynchpin: Spectrographs as DNA

Eerie Similarity

I noticed that these two images were eerily similar: a spectrograph of the sun and a visualization of the human genome.

DNA visualization

Solar spectrograph

The striking patterns in both DNA sequencing and stellar spectrographs reveal encoded information that defines the essential nature of their respective entities.

Meme Machines

In the school of Richard Dawkins, we are all "meme machines." In other words, we are vessels for structures of information, our DNA, to propagate. DNA isn't just our unique fingerprint; it is our essential being. The signature IS the organism.

Stellar Identity

In the same way, a star is its spectrum. The spectrograph isn't just a measurement of a star—it is the star's essential signature, its "DNA."

Mutation

Elements combine in the sun's core, creating new atomic structures in a process analogous to genetic mutation.

Reproduction

Heavy elements scatter in supernovae, and stellar lineage can be traced through these elements. Research has shown that we can "interrogate" stars to find their lost siblings through their spectral "DNA."

Further Implications

Memetic Universality

Atoms are also memetic structures, not fundamental elements. ALL recurring structures in the universe are likely to be mimetic.

A New Paradigm

This theory invites us to reconsider our definition of life beyond carbon-based biochemistry. By viewing stars as systems capable of evolution through natural selection, we may finally break free from the last vestiges of parochialism and understand that life—in some form—may be a universal property of the cosmos.
