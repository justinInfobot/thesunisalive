The Sun is Alive

Darwin's theory of evolution for stars

What if stars, like our sun, are not merely nuclear furnaces but complex living systems evolving over cosmic timescales? This theory proposes a new framework for understanding stellar bodies through the lens of evolutionary biology.

Key Observations

  • Timescale Perception: The main reason we can't perceive the sun as alive is because of the vast difference in timescales between human and stellar existence.
  • Stable Complexity: The sun exhibits both remarkable stability and internal complexity, hallmarks of living systems.
  • Genetic Parallels: Stellar spectrographs may be analogous to DNA, containing information about stellar lineage and evolution.
  • Memetic Structures: Atoms may be memetic structures rather than fundamental elements, suggesting recurring patterns throughout the universe are mimetic.
  • Homeostasis: Stars demonstrate homeostatic properties, maintaining internal balance despite changing external conditions.

Fascinating Facts

  • The sun produces less heat per unit volume than the human body.
  • Once a photon is emitted by the sun's core, it takes at least 10,000 years to reach the surface, revealing complex internal dynamics.
  • Sun spots, weather, currents, and field shifts are all part of the sun's complex system, much like the internal processes of living organisms.

Implications

If stars are indeed alive in some sense, our understanding of the cosmos transforms dramatically. The Fermi paradox becomes less puzzling when we consider that we may already be surrounded by non-carbon-based life forms operating on entirely different timescales.
